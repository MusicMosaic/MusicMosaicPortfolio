Pop Music Genre


Definition of Pop Music

Pop music (from the English pop music, a contraction of popular music), also known simply as pop, is a genre of popular music that originated in the late 1950s as a derivation of traditional pop, in combination with other musical genres that were fashionable at that time.

Watch Billie Eilish - bad guy

Hip Hop Music Genre


Definition of Hip Hop Music

Dance music style born in the United States of America in the 1970s as a derivative of funk and characterized by its electronic base and for being associated with alternative manifestations such as break dance or graffiti. It uses a technique named "Rapping" which is poetry spoken rhythmically.

Watch Eminem - Without Me

Rock Music Genre


Definition of Rock Music

Rock music is a style born in the 1960s as a derivative of rock and roll and characterized by the use of complex melodies and rhythms, with fairly fixed instrumentation (basically electric guitar, electric bass, keyboard and drums) and with an energetic rhythm underlined by the power of the bass and the drums, all of which is often linked to an anti-authoritarian and provocative attitude; it has developed a multitude of variants and has given way to new styles, such as pop, punk or heavy.

Watch Metallica: The Unforgiven II

Rhythm and Blues Music Genre


Definition of Rhythm and Blues Music

Musical style born in the 1940s as a derivative of blues and related to jazz, soul and gospel; It is characterized by being a mainly vocal genre, typical of solo singers or grouped in quartets or quintets that accompany their voice with the interventions of a choir.

Watch Gary B.B. Coleman - The Sky is Crying

Country Music Genre


Definition of Country Music

Country (also called country & western, country music or country music) is a musical genre that emerged in the 1920s in the rural regions of the South of the United States and in the Maritimes of Canada and Australia. In its origins, it combined the folk music of some European immigrant countries, mainly Ireland, with other musical forms, such as blues, bluegrass and spiritual and religious music, such as gospel.

Watch Shaboozey - A Bar Song (Tipsy)

Funk Music Genre


Definition of Funk Music

Funk is a vibrant and rhythmic music genre that emerged in the mid-1960s, blending elements of soul, jazz, and R&B. Characterized by its strong bass lines, syncopated rhythms, and emphasis on groove, funk music focuses on creating a danceable beat that invites movement. Key features include the use of horns, electric guitars, and intricate drum patterns, often accompanied by catchy melodies and energetic vocals.

Watch George Clinton - We Got the Funk

Folk Music Genre


Definition of Folk Music

Folk music is a genre that encompasses the traditional songs and melodies of a particular culture or community, often passed down orally through generations. It reflects the everyday life, experiences, and values of the people, frequently addressing themes such as love, work, nature, and social issues. Folk music is characterized by its simplicity and authenticity, often featuring acoustic instruments like guitars, banjos, fiddles, and harmonicas.

Watch Balochi Folk - Mumtaz Sabzal

Jazz Music Genre


Definition of Jazz Music

Jazz is a vibrant and dynamic music genre that originated in the early 20th century in the United States, primarily among African American communities in New Orleans. Characterized by its unique blend of African rhythms, blues, ragtime, and European musical traditions, jazz emphasizes improvisation, syncopation, and expressive melodies.

Watch Michael Bublé - Feeling Good

Disco Music Genre


Definition of Disco Music

Disco music (also known in English disco music or, in French, discothèque) is a dance music genre whose popularity peaked in the mid-1970s. Latin Americans and psychedelic music connoisseurs, as well as other communities in New York City and Philadelphia during the 1970s.

Watch Boney M. - Rasputin

Classical Music Genre


Definition of Classical Music

Classical music is a broad genre that encompasses a wide range of Western art music composed over several centuries, typically from the medieval period to the present day. It is characterized by its structured forms, complex harmonies, and orchestration, often emphasizing the written score rather than improvisation.

Watch Beethoven's 5th Symphony

Electronic Music Genre


Definition of Electronic Music

Electronic music is a broad genre that primarily involves the use of electronic devices and technology to create sound. Emerging in the mid-20th century, it encompasses a wide range of styles, including techno, house, trance, drum and bass, and ambient music. Characterized by synthesized sounds, beats, and samples, electronic music often features rhythmic patterns and textures that distinguish it from traditional acoustic genres.

Watch J. Balvin, Skrillex - In Da Getto

Traditional Music Genre


Definition of Traditional Music

Traditional music refers to the folk music that has been passed down through generations within a specific culture or community, often reflecting the history, values, and customs of that group. This genre typically includes songs, dances, and instrumental pieces that are tied to cultural rituals, celebrations, and daily life.

Watch Coke Studio Season 9 | Paar Chanaa De | Shilpa Rao & Noori

Metal Music Genre


Definition of Heavy Metal Music

Heavy metal is a genre of rock music characterized by its loud, aggressive sound, heavy use of distorted electric guitars, powerful drumming, and strong vocals, often with a theatrical or rebellious edge. Emerging in the late 1960s and early 1970s, heavy metal drew influences from blues rock, psychedelic rock, and hard rock. Key elements include complex guitar solos, dark themes, and a strong emphasis on rhythm.

Watch Slipknot - Psychosocial

Camera Angles and Movements


The way the camera is used is extremely important in producing meaning within a music video. The angles and movements can help create different effects. The shots are what make the video unique. Below are different shots and movements:

  • Very Wide Shot: Used to show the audience where the artist is, but the emphasis is still on the artist in front of the setting or location.
  • Wide Shot: Focuses on the artist while still allowing the audience to see the location of the video.
  • Mid Shot: Shows the artist from their waist to the head, focusing mainly on the subject but still giving a feel for the location and setting.
  • Medium Shot: Halfway between a mid shot and a close-up, showing the artist’s chest and head.
  • Close-up: Shows the artist’s face or an object in detail; this shot can reveal more about the mise-en-scène as facial expressions, color, and makeup contribute towards the video.
  • Extreme Close-up: Focuses closely on the artist’s face (e.g., eyes, nose, lips) to identify something or to add meaning and effect to the video. It can also show that the video is lip-synced.
  • Over-the-Shoulder Shot: This shot is effective in narrative-based music videos as it gives the perspective of the artist's view while including them in it. It can also be used as a shot-reverse-shot if the artist is having a conversation with someone.
  • Cut-ins: Often used to match the lyrics within the music video. If something is said within a song, for example, an object is described, the object may then come into view.

Just like movements, camera angles significantly impact the meaning and style of the music video. Wherever a camera is placed can show the power of the artist/characters or sometimes show an exact location from which we should be seeing it. By choosing different angles, the viewers gain different perspectives on the action, which makes it more interesting.

An example of this is "Salute" by Little Mix, which showcases various angles and movements, particularly from a low angle, demonstrating the power the girls need to overcome issues of equality. The music video features a lot of different angles and movements, particularly from a low angle view, which helps convey the message of empowerment.


Camera Movements

Different camera movements can be used to add effects to music videos. Examples of camera movements are:

  • Pans: This occurs when the camera scans the scene horizontally. The camera is usually placed on a tripod, and the camera is turned to follow the object which is typically situated in the middle of the screen.
  • Dolly or Tracking Shots: This is where the camera is mounted on a cart that travels along a smooth track to follow the action.

Just like in any other form of video media, certain camera movements are used for different reasons; an establishing shot will generally set the scene at the beginning; a long shot will normally set out the location of a closer scene, allowing the audience to better understand the atmosphere; mid shots and close-ups will be used to see the artist's face and emotions depending on the music style.

For example, "Hollaback Girl" by Gwen Stefani features various tracking and panning shots which are both slow and fast. This gives the music video a dynamic energy because they are always on the move, presenting the artist in a lively way and providing a visual narrative that keeps the audience engaged.

Editing Techniques

When editing, various techniques are utilized to enhance the video and give it a professional feel. Common editing techniques include transitions such as fade to black, green screen effects, and adjustments to lighting, contrast, and brightness.

Glossary of Cuts

  • Cutting on Action: Cutting while there is movement on-screen.
  • Cutaway: Cutting to an insert shot and then back.
  • Cross-Cutting: Back and forth between locations (e.g., phone call).
  • Jump Cuts: Disjointed edits in the same shot or action.
  • Match Cut: Cut from one shot to a similar shot by matching the action or composition.

Glossary of Transitions

  • Fade in/out: Transition to/from black.
  • Dissolve: Blend shots together.
  • Smash Cut: Abrupt transition from one scene to another.
  • Iris: Old-fashioned transition; a circle that opens and closes in the camera.
  • Wipes: Screen wipes from one side to the other.
  • Invisible Cuts: Create the impression of a single take.
  • L-cut: Audio transition where the sound from the next scene starts before you see it.
  • J-cut: Audio of the next scene starts before you transition to the visual.

Sound

Music videos incorporate two types of sound: diegetic and non-diegetic. Diegetic sound is naturally present in the video, while non-diegetic sound is added during editing. Sound can be edited to create different effects and variations in the artist's vocals.

Key Sound Terminology

  • Soundtrack: The recorded sound element of a film.
  • Theme Music/Tune: A recurrent melody in the film.
  • Sound Effects: Sounds other than dialogue or music made artificially.
  • Ambient Sound: Background noise and surrounding environmental sounds.
  • Dialogue: Speech.
  • Voiceover: Narration in a film not synchronized with the image of the speaker.
  • Direct Address: When characters speak directly to the camera/audience.
  • Diegetic Sound: Any sound that has an onscreen source and belongs to the world of the film (e.g., dialogues and atmospheric sounds).
  • Non-Diegetic Sound: Any sound that does not have an onscreen source and is not heard by the characters (e.g., some voiceovers, music).
  • Sound Bridges: Any sounds that continue from one shot to another, enhancing the continuity of the film.
  • Parallel Sound: Sound that complements the image track, reflecting each other.
  • Contrapuntal Sound: Sound that does not complement or fit with the image track.

Mise-En-Scène

The mise-en-scène for music videos varies with the genre, consisting of the following components:

  • Lighting: How the scene is lit.
  • Props: Objects and items within the frame.
  • Costume: What the artist is wearing.
  • Hair and Makeup: The style of the artist.
  • Location: Where the music video is filmed.
  • Colour: Specific colors representing certain moods and atmospheres.
